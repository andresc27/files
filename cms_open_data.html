High Energy Physics and Data Science

Introduction

High-energy physics (HEP) explores the fundamental components of the universe by studying particle collisions at extreme energies. These experiments help physicists test the Standard Model, search for new particles, and investigate the fundamental forces of nature.

At the Compact Muon Solenoid (CMS) experiment at CERN, advanced detectors are used to analyze the particles produced in collisions at the Large Hadron Collider (LHC). In particular, muon detectors play a key role in identifying and measuring muons, which are crucial for many physics analyses. CMS utilizes a combination of Drift Tubes (DTs), Resistive Plate Chambers (RPCs), and Cathode Strip Chambers (CSCs) to ensure precise muon detection across different regions of the detector.

Given the immense volume of data generated by the LHC, **data science techniques** are essential for processing, filtering, and analyzing high-energy physics events. Machine learning and statistical tools help extract meaningful insights from petabytes of collision data, enabling physicists to uncover new phenomena and refine our understanding of the universe.

Particle Physics and the Importance of Finding Top Quarks

Particle physics studies the fundamental particles and forces that make up the universe. The top quark is the heaviest known elementary particle and plays a crucial role in understanding the Standard Model. Its interactions, particularly with the Higgs boson, provide insights into fundamental forces and potential new physics.

Using CMS Open Data and Its Importance

This study utilized CMS Open Data , publicly available data from the Compact Muon Solenoid (CMS) experiment at CERN's Large Hadron Collider (LHC). Using real collision data allows researchers worldwide to validate theoretical models, train new analysis techniques, and promote transparency in high-energy physics. Open Data provides an opportunity for reproducible research and encourages broader participation in fundamental physics.

Tools Used for Data Analysis and Extraction
  • The study analyzed proton-proton collisions at 13 TeV using CMS Open Data.
  • Data processing and extraction were performed using Python, with libraries such as:
    • Uproot and Coffea for handling large datasets.
    • Pandas and NumPy for data manipulation.
    • Matplotlib and Seaborn for visualization.
Boosted Decision Tree (BDT) Simplification

To distinguish between signal (four top quarks) and background (e.g., tt̄, W+jets), a Boosted Decision Tree (BDT) algorithm was applied. This machine learning technique improves classification by weighting difficult-to-classify events, enhancing signal detection.

The BDT was implemented using XGBoost and Scikit-learn, leveraging gradient boosting to optimize event classification.

Workflow
  1. Data Selection: Events were filtered based on electron + jets criteria.
  2. Event Reconstruction: Identification of electrons, jets, and b-tagged jets.
  3. Monte Carlo Simulations: Used to compare experimental data with theoretical models.
  4. BDT Application: Machine learning was used to refine signal-background discrimination.
  5. Validation: Agreement between CMS Open Data and Monte Carlo simulations was verified.
Results
  • The analysis successfully replicated key findings from previous studies on four-top-quark production.
  • The BDT classifier improved the separation between signal and background.
  • The study provides a framework for using CMS Open Data in future research.

The analysis can be run using the swan tool provided by CERN. You must be a CMS member.
