CMS Open Data Workshop

Introduction

The CMS Open Data Workshop provides an introduction to high-energy physics data analysis using real collision data from the Compact Muon Solenoid (CMS) experiment at CERN. This workshop aims to make scientific data accessible to a broader audience, including students, researchers, and educators, by teaching them how to use CMS Open Data to conduct meaningful analyses.

Participants learn essential tools and methodologies, including data selection, processing, and visualization techniques, using software frameworks such as ROOT and Python. The hands-on exercises guide users through working with real particle physics data and extracting physical observables from reconstructed events.

The Importance of CMS Open Data

CMS Open Data plays a crucial role in advancing transparency and reproducibility in high-energy physics. By making experimental data publicly available, the CMS collaboration allows independent researchers and students to explore real collision events, validate scientific results, and develop new analysis techniques.

Workflow of the Workshop

The workshop follows a structured workflow that includes:

  • Introduction to CMS Open Data and data formats
  • Hands-on experience with data selection and event filtering
  • Analysis of reconstructed particles, including electrons and muons
  • Visualization and interpretation of physics results

Results and Findings

The participants successfully extracted key physics variables from the data, including electron and muon kinematic properties. Using the PhysObjectExtractorTool, they analyzed particle properties and studied fundamental physics processes.

My Contribution

I contributed to the workshop by extracting key physics variables for muon and electron objects. Specifically, I worked on modifying the electron_analyser and muon_analyser files in the PhysObjectExtractorTool. These modifications enabled a more detailed study of particle kinematics and event selection.

To achieve this, I utilized C++ along with the ROOT framework, which is widely used in high-energy physics for data analysis. My tasks included:

  • Extracting transverse momentum (pT), pseudorapidity (η), and energy variables.
  • Extracting additional variables such as electron veto, medium, tight, MVA, and SIP3D, which are crucial for advanced particle identification and background rejection.
  • Debugging and optimizing C++ code for efficient data extraction.

Additionally, I contributed to editing the tutorial site, ensuring that the instructional materials were clear and accessible for participants.
