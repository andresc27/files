CMS Open Data Workshop

Introduction

The CMS Open Data Workshop provides researchers, students, and enthusiasts with the opportunity to explore real high-energy physics (HEP) data from the Compact Muon Solenoid (CMS) experiment at CERN. The workshop introduces participants to key concepts in particle physics, data analysis techniques, and software tools used in CMS research.

By working with publicly available data, attendees can gain hands-on experience in reconstructing and analyzing particle collision events, learning how to extract meaningful physics insights using computational methods.

The Importance of CMS Open Data

The CMS experiment generates vast amounts of data from proton-proton collisions at the Large Hadron Collider (LHC). Sharing this data with the public fosters transparency, collaboration, and new scientific discoveries beyond the original research teams.

The CMS Open Data initiative allows researchers and educators worldwide to explore fundamental physics, validate previous findings, and develop new analysis techniques. It also serves as an invaluable resource for training future physicists and data scientists in handling large-scale scientific datasets.

Workflow and Results

The workshop guides participants through the essential steps of CMS data analysis:

  • Understanding the CMS detector and its components.
  • Accessing and processing open datasets.
  • Applying event selection criteria to isolate relevant physics processes.
  • Extracting and analyzing key variables such as electron and muon properties.
  • Visualizing and interpreting results to uncover particle interactions.

In particular, the PhysObjectExtractorTool was used to extract essential variables from CMS data, focusing on the electron_analyser and muon_analyser modules. These analyses help characterize the behavior of electrons and muons, crucial components in studies of the Standard Model and beyond.

The results obtained through this workflow enable researchers to reconstruct key events, compare findings with theoretical predictions, and contribute to ongoing high-energy physics studies.
